Synopsis

In a galaxy far away, there is a kid without innate ability to practice internal techniques. So, in order to gain the respect of his father, he resolutely chooses to follow the more difficult and painful path of practicing external techniques. As the years go by, he grows up, but what really changes his life is a mysterious meteoric crystal stone – the Meteoric Tear. This stone fuses with the young man’s body unnoticed, and he seems to undergo drastic transformations as a result. After that, everything is changed. Eventually his father knows that the son for whom he hasn’t really shown a lot of consideration possesses astonishing abilities. And there’s a lot more to come.

(Source: Novel Updates)

In this review, I will treat both "seasons", as they are split in MAL, as one work. It essentially is, and it's especially relevant since the end of the first "season" ends up in a ridiculous cliff hanger. Long story short, I do not recommend it.

The highlight of Xingchen Bian occurs in the first 6 episodes by far. The protagonist, Qin Yu, is born without cultivation powers, and, in an effort to gain the attention of his father, practices physical martial arts, which is said to be far more difficult than cultivation skills, such that there has only been a few experts in the entire history. The regimen is strict -- think of Rock Lee's taijutsu from Naruto -- but Qin Yu struggles his way through it, and we could really feel the effort he puts into training and empathize with his motivations, as his father essentially relegated him to a country-side palace, alone. The show excels in several montage sequences, with Chinese pop music playing in the background, truly capturing the emotions of those scenes.

However, it goes downhill from there. Half way through, the plot devolves into a more stereotypical shounen, when the protagonist "finds" some asspull magical artifacts that precludes him from ever having to work hard again. Let me be clear. He still works, but the weakly explained magical powers renders it so that his development is no longer the sole consequence of his talent and efforts, but some external event. Shounen is no stranger to this kind of device; in fact, it happens with Nine-Tails and Naruto's bloodline, the significance of "D" of Luffy, Ichigo's Hollow form, etc.

As a taijutsu practitioner, Qin Yu is underestimated and his father continues to ignore him. So at night, he dresses up in an Assassin's Creed cosplay and kills villains on the DL like Robin Hood, and we still get some drama along those lines. The action throughout the show is interspersed with some motion capture to make the movement seem smooth. I don't really have any complaints about the art and animation quality overall, if you could deal with the fact that it's 3D. Given its age, it's better than average.

Here's why I don't recommend it. The end of the first season, without giving much away, leads into a deus ex machina scene, sloppily directed for the purpose of forcing the plot into another direction. There is a battle, and none of the characters' actions make any sense, neither the protagonists' nor villains. With Qin Yu's artifact, he becomes the strongest fighter introduced in the series. That's quite natural in this kind of show, but not at his pace. It's like he entered a cheat code or bought a level 60 character.

But that's not the most insulting part of it. Perhaps due to power creep and the author running out of ideas in writing a touching story, perhaps the father and son drama played out, but the second season takes a turn for the bizarre and fantastical. Qin Yu and his bird, while exploring the vast world of cultivation, end up learning how to act exactly like they're in the air while underwater, except once in awhile remembering to swim when it's convenient for the plot. In this section, he fights long and flashy CG fights against different kinds of sea snakes and crabs, which is to be honest, despite its action, utterly boring, lacking any of the dramatic thrust that motivated the first 6 episodes of the first season. He is no longer bound by his insecurities toward his father, and no longer has any motive to prove himself or live up to his responsibilities in society. There is no more reason for the protagonist to be alive.

Strangely, this part feels more like a Chinese take on the Little Mermaid, complete with an obese octopus that looks like Ursula (the villain), a pair of sharks that look like the Street Sharks (Ninja Turtles rip-off), half-naked sea creatures taking the form of human servant girls, etc. It takes a couple episodes dealing with a bizarre retelling of a Monkey King (Son Goku) parable, and then a couple more with Qin Yu gawking at girls and awkwardly simping, before just moving on.

In other words, everything that's good about it falls apart.

And if you want to watch a show without a decent end, and will not have a decent end, because it's a string already fraying before any semblance of a reasonable finish, then perhaps this is your case study to demonstrate how something decent could run out of steam midway.

I was planning to write a review on this anime anyways, but after reading the only other review, I now have to write one.... (the other review was really bad... if you couldn't tell already)

This review is spoiler free so feel free to read it before you watch.

Xing Chen Bian is a Chinese anime I found while going though the romance genre on MAL. I decided to give it a go since it had a decent rating. With that being said, there are zero romance in this anime, ZERO. so if romance is the only thing you care about, move along cuz you ain't finding it here.

Let's start with the story, it's pretty basic. We all have seen an anime where the main character is told that he is hopeless and can't do great things, and somehow he becomes OP. Well, this is one of those. The main character Qin, Yu was born without the special gift to hold power within himself, so he was deemed useless. Although his father never gave up on him and loved him very much, he didn't have high hopes for him. The story begins with a flash back (or I guess flash foreword) of a bit of the last episode, and then cuts back to Yu's childhood.

The plot itself wasn't bad but it lacks creativity in the bigger picture (6/10)

The music used in this anime was great as with almost all Chinese anime, they add on to the mood and gives the scenes which have music more character. The background soundtrack as well as the sound affects are also very well done, nothing felt out of place. (9/10)

This anime uses CGI combined with real human motion capture, so while the animation is presented in 3D, everything is very smooth and sometimes felt real. It is not perfect however as some scenes still feels edited. Overall the art was nice to look at so (9/10)

Now on to some of my small nit-picks. The pacing felt off on the earlier half of the anime, some things I felt could have been cut shorter and vice versa, however I understand that could just be personal reference. The dialog sometime have unnecessary pauses and charters just kinda of stood there doing nothing. There was many times were it felt more like cut-scenes from a RPG rather than an anime. But hey, these are just some of the things I found odd.

Last but not least, the translation. I was born in China and lived there for 12 years before I moved to Canada, so my first language is Chinese. Chinese as a language in nature is more complicated than English, so any Chinese anime you watch will be better if you understood Chinese. As this anime takes place in a fantasy version of China a few hundred years ago, the language used is sometimes complicated and when translated, lose some of its meaning. So just keep that in mind if the translation doesn't make sense sometimes.

Overall, this was a fairly enjoyable anime to watch. It is definitely not a masterpiece or something close to it, but if you just want something to watch and pass the time, this anime is not a bad choice. Overall a 7.2 out of 10 in my books.

Give it a go and see what you think!

This is a really good anime with its backstory stemming from the Qin dynasty which was China's first dynasty. This is a story that follows both fiction and non-fiction. Most of what was in the first season are historical events. However, there are some changes to fit the story of a mythic legend named Yu Qin. As the story progresses, Yu Qin becomes stronger and in season 2 meets a well-known Chinese legend, Sun Wu Kong. Well in the anime he wasn't named that but his weapon and looks are inspired by Sun Wu Kong. This has roots in Chinese epics such as Xi You Ji. I think that this anime is really good as a native speaker, however, there is a bit of loss in translation in English so I had to wholly watched it in Chinese. I do believe that this is a phenomenal Chinese anime that sees Yu Qin's growth as he seeks to become a god through his trials and tribulations.
I do believe that this is anime is a 9/10 all around for me. Do give it a try.
