Synopsis

Using state of the art animation End Of Summer tells the story of one young man's relationship with five women in a frank and sensual manner seldom explored in even the most daring of live action films.

Although the hero, Wataru, wants only to score with Mai, the girl of his dreams, he must first deal with four other young women scattered in his path like land mines. There is infatuated and juvenile Miho, tomboyish athlete Misa, naive Kurumi, and the caustic Satomi, who works in the restaurant where Wataru hangs out and whose personal life become hopelessly intertwined with Wataru's.

The inevitable liaison between Satomi and Wataru sets a new standard in the rendering of animated erotica and may forever change the way animation is viewed in North America.

Haiku review:

There's a solution
to ev'ry problem; it's called
taking her to bed

And because after two whole days the haiku review has not become standard, the long, drawn-out version:

Oh, for a world in which every young lady's every problem could be solved by taking her to bed. It's something to strive toward -- if you're the guy doing the bedding.

That's the deep philosophical and spiritual premise of "Doukyuusei: Natsu no Owari ni," a 1994 ecchi (then and sometimes now it's considered hentai, though it does not meet the definition because no genitals are shown). It is not something you'll easily find, and it's not something you're likely to want to spend a lot of time seeking out.

But it does have its charms, not least being the hilariously inept translation in the subtitles (which are presented in a curlicue typeface -- kind of bizarre). It's not just anywhere that you'll find the eager Wataru (aforementioned bedder) saying to the flower girl, "Make a bunch of flowers of 3000 dollars for me." (Don't worry -- he "helps" the flower girl, too, before it's all over.)

One young lady's concern was, as she put it, "I just want to be an adult quickly, please."

As the great boy himself put it, "The affair between man and woman is always tangled up." Said one young woman who may believe Wataru is the personification of divine intervention: "I really thanks for the arrangement of God."

But all is not well. There's a girl he really likes, Mai, but her father has hired a bodyguard to dissuade Wataru. Said the bodyguard, "Get away with your tail quickly!" Who could argue with that? But brave Wataru soldiers on and takes her to an amusement park, where she is not happy. Quoth she, "My dad is the boss of here."

This is all against a backdrop of playful young badinage, as in the girls' shower: "Sachi, Your breast turns big recently." Such writing is sure to steal your heart, no?

Not that Wataru's life is itself trouble-free. He admits, "I disgust the school which we have to study a lot."

There is much to think about, such as what "Don't gift me, MY SON" could possibly mean. The question may never be answered, and we are all diminished because of it.

You might think that that's all, but Wataru has more help to dispense. He is gentle and caring; he advises another young lady (not his heart's desire, but his other organs are not as discerning): "If you move carelessly this way, the dress will be wrecked."

He does some of his best work when it's an act of charity. "Please! Help me forget him," asks a young lady, and it's mission accomplished when he's helped her brains out.

But his heart belongs to Mai, of whom it is said, "She is the golden sportswoman who bears the reputation of the school." Will he ever find true love? Certainly not before he whispers this gently into the ear of a different, more adventurous girl: "Let's go back. I release the rope for you now." She is grateful, gushing: "Next time, surely I can do it without tying up."

So, yes, my children, the story does have a plot. Will Wataru live to hear young Mai whisper, "My body can feel it"? Or will he fold when the bodyguard advises him, "You better leave her farther, in order to not getting in trouble"?

Ah, but that would spoil the whole thing, wouldn't it.

This may seem a little tongue-in-cheek, but as ecchi/borderline hentai goes, the series is surprisingly watchable. Every so often there's anime that's funny for unintended reasons. Because it's unintentionally amusing it can take on a new kind of charm. "Doukyuusei: Natsu no Owari ni" is one of those. Taken seriously, it's so-so at best. But embraced along with its flaws, it's almost sweet.
