Synopsis

Fifth-grader, Toriyasu, and his little sister, Meeko, have a dog named Papadoll who has been missing for the past week. One night, two cats take the two children to the distant cat land of Banipal Witt, where Papadoll was abducted. Because of his long exposure to Banipal Witt's sun, Papadoll mutated into a giant dog monster, terrorizing the city under the control of the evil Princess Buburina, who has the curse of turning anyone she touches into balloons. Toriyasu, Meeko, and the cats have until the next sunrise to turn Papadoll and the ballooned citizens of Banipal Witt back to normal and return to their home planet—or end up with the same fate as their dog.

(Source: AniDB)

Totsuzen! Neko no Kuni Banipal Witt is a 1995 film brought to us by our old friends at Studio Hibari, whom you may recall from their work on Kashimashi & Venus Versus Virus. So, how do these folks handle a children's film and is it going to be any better than their track record would indicate? Let's give it a shot and delve in.

Story:

Papadoll has gone missing. His smallest human, Meeko, is convinced that he's been kidnapped by aliens but her brother, Toriyasu, doesn't take her seriously. In fact, the little brat doesn't seem to care saying that Papadoll just lay about all day anyway. That's when Meeko notices a strange cat observing them. That night, three strange cats burst into their room, wanting to take Toriyasu away. Meeko insists on going along.

The big flaw with the film on a narrative level is that it's paced pretty awkwardly. You get a long, kind of boring scene where the characters plan their next move intermixed with scenes of what the antagonists are doing and ending with an action sequence that's welcome, at first, but just drags after a while And the film is barely an hour and a quarter long so the way the scenes still manage to drag, even if just a bit, is kind of a big deal.

On the positive side, the concept behind this is fairly creative. I also do like that it makes a big theme of Toriyasu being forced to come to terms with his own behaviour and how it's contributed to the problem. There aren't many works of children's media that demand that level of self-reflection.

Characters:

The characters vary a bit as well. Some of the dynamics come across as contrived and don't work very well. The whole conflict turned into a kind of creepily close respect between Toriyasu & ChuChu being the prime example. A lot of the side characters are also pretty one-note. However, I do have to give the film some credit. Toriyasu's character arc works quite well. The antagonist, Buburina, is pretty terrible but she's not just the purely evil villain you get in a lot of children's media. She does have redeeming qualities. She may not be at the level of a Ghibli villain, but it does give her some dimension.

Art:

The art also varies. One the negative side, the character designs don't look very good. That being said, a lot of the backgrounds are creative and interesting. The whole world of Banipal Witt looks like a drug-induced hallucination. It's almost like Hibari took visual inspiration from Apple Films' opus, Yellow Submarine. It's a very strange looking world, but one with a lot of atmosphere. The film's action scenes also work quite nicely.

Sound:

The cast is pretty decent. Hiroaki Hori, Sasaki Mirai & Hidaka Noriko all do quite well. Saegusa Shigeaki handles the music and it works nicely enough.

Ho-yay:

Don't expect any. To be fair, an emphasis on romance really wouldn't work for the film in general.

Final Thoughts:

Totsuzen! Neko no Kuni Banipal Witt was actually pretty enjoyable. Yes, it could have been better, particularly in some areas. However, it's also creative and interesting enough in its own right. My final rating is going to be a 7/10. Tomorrow I'll end this year's film festival week with a look at Mononoke Hime.

Totsuzen! Neko no Kuni Banipal Witt, also known as Catnapped! The Movie, is an extremely charming children's movie about the secret world of cats. I was extremely fortunate to find this movie at Media Play (I've had this movie for THAT long...Media Play was still in business! xD) and I've been hooked ever since.

This is definitely one to bring home to the kids. The storyline is simple and fun for kids, and the animation is simple but entertaining. There are plenty of humorous moments to cheer on the progress, and once you hear the ending theme song, Yume He No Tobira, I challenge you to try to get it out of your head! :D

If you're a Miyazaki fan (though, who isn't?), though this isn't a Miyazaki creation, Catnapped! The Movie is a fantasy-based film centered around animals, which Miyazaki is well known for. It's also got the same sort of magical action sequences we've come to love and expect from Miyazaki's films. However, if you only seem to find yourself in the more dark, dramatic animes, this may not be the film for you; It is a film intended for children so it has cheerful elements.

Don't forget to add Totsuzen! Neko no Kuni Banipal Witt to your Queue! This is a thoroughly enjoyable film I think you'll come to love. Enjoy!

It's not uncommon for someone to recommend a children's movie for the "young at heart", not to mention that many enjoy

children's films better as adults. My case with this movie is truly no exception, as I find it's a very cute and heartwarming

movie that very few people know exists... and I didn't see it until I was a junior in high school.

PREMISE:
The film is about a brother and sister named Toriyasu and Meeko whose dog Papadoll has been missing for a long time. It

turns out that the dog has been taken to Banipal Witt, a strange world populated by anthropomorphic cats, where the evil

princess Buburina and her henchman Doh-Doh plan to use it for world domination.

Yes, this film has a weird premise and you might think "seen it before", but even if you end up watching it, skepticism

notwithstanding, you'll probably be surprised by all the creativity put into it. In fact, this film is a perfect example of a weird

movie that actually has a coherent plot, and I'll get to why in a moment.

WRITING:
While some of the story ideas might be cliché, credit should be given where it's due. While many good works of fiction have

a character you hate because of what they do or the way they are written comes off as annoying, not one character in this

film is unlikeable, even the bad guys! *SPOILER ALERT* For example, while Toriyasu starts off as moody and lazy, he gets

better later on, not to mention his sister Meeko, despite the fate she ends up with, comes off as brave and smart and even

helps the audience understand the villain.

The villains, despite clearly posing a threat with Doh-Doh controlling Papadoll to abduct the residents of Banipal Witt and

Buburina's curse to turn whatever living thing she touches into a balloon, are also impossible to truly hate. For one thing, a

flashback showing how Buburina got the curse and even exposition stating how Doh-Doh ended up the way he did are given

to the audience along with several other tidbits of information that help you understand the story. It's rare that villains in a

work of fiction aimed at kids are ever written in a way that makes the audience sympathize with them or understand how

they turned as it's mostly just "he or she's bad, therefore they must die!" morality in these films, so it's no surprise this film

passes in this respect.

In short, the film takes its time revealing information to the audience by explaining some of its things with dialogue and

others with visuals and leaving some details out so that you can use what you see and hear or take the time to think about

to put all the pieces together, something I find unusual for a children's story, let alone an anime.

Also, it's worth noting that among the messages the film teaches its audience, it tells them that violence is not OK, it's

wrong to take your anger out on someone in a way that's harmful and that love, caring and selflessness are important in a

way that is subtle rather than hammering it in, something I found truly surprising in an anime, especially one aimed at kids.

ART:
The animation is, for the most part, very fluid and astounding for an anime production, although there is the occasional

low-framerate or repeated animation. The fluidity of the animation matches the zaniness and charm of Banipal Witt and

never fails to amaze with its sometimes frightening or creative imagery in a way that is reminiscent of Yellow Submarine and

Little Nemo: Adventures in Slumberland.

The character designs, naturally, include cuteness. While the cats themselves are humanoid and some look more human

than others (particularly Buburina and Chu-Chu), the two children look average for a children's anime but after they're

transformed into cats, they may come off as irresistably cute (saw-toothed as they are). From a design standpoint, I liked

Meeko, Chu-Chu, Suttoboke, Hoi-Hoi, Sandada and Buburina.

Not only is the world bright and candy-colored to go along with the madness, but there is also a unique day-and-night

system with its own magic and even the use of old transportation, including hot-air balloons and blimps. By contrast, the

parts with the children in Japan look more subdued, which helps make the zaniness all the more unavoidable.

As far as animation is concerned, my favorite scenes are the ones with Lily Remlon's dance, the scene where Buburina

turns her prisoners into balloons and the escape from Buburina's castle and the subsequent air battle due to all the detail

and effort put into each and every scene.

The stop-motion in the opening and closing credits, while different from the film itself, earned a mention because they help

prepare the audience for fun and prompt them to wait until it's over before shutting off respectively, what with creativity and

weirdness... and because I'm a sucker for stop-motion, but I digress. However, it brings me to my next point...

SOUND:
The music in this film is mostly limited to the time spent in Banipal Witt, which helps make you enjoy the world it takes

place in a lot more, especially because it has awesome music that fits the scene it's in.

The theme song "Yume e No Tobira" deserves a mention not only because it is very light-hearted and catchy but also

because of Mayumi Iizuka's beautiful singing voice. Plus, it mentions friendship, fun and imagination, making its placement

in the film all the more perfect (as I'm writing this, I'm listening to it for the second time).

As for the voice work, I've seen both the subtitled and dubbed versions, and I have this to say. I actually like the dubbed

version better somewhat. While the voices for Meeko in both versions can be grating (to be fair, she is a small child), the

dub actress at least doesn't scream, though it is more high-pitched.

However, both versions have some things in common. Specifically, Noriko Hidaka and Mary Elizabeth McGlynn ham up their

performances as Buburina (the former especially, even reaching Miranda Richardson levels of overacting at times), helping

make her impossible to hate. Mayumi Iizuka and Lia Sargent both match the mild Tsundere personality of Chu-Chu by

coming off as initially stuck-up but later warming up. Those voices I just mentioned are probably the main things that prevent

me from hating the original version.

OVERALL:

I was pleasantly surprised by this movie, despite having found many spoilers online prior to watching, and it saddens me

that it's not very well-known. Like some, I heard about it through a trailer on a Sailor Moon VHS and due to the weirdness

often giving me bad dreams, it took years to gain the confidence to watch it and I don't regret it.

On the surface, the film looks cutesy yet bizarre and nightmarish, but dig deeper and you'll find a heartwarming film with

something to learn, likeable characters and brilliant animation.

If you love cute things, children's films, anime and especially cats, I highly recommend it.
